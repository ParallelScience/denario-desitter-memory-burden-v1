The current framework assumes a uniform distribution of memory modes across all $N_s$ species. I hypothesize that the "stability corridor" and the tension in the tensor-to-scalar ratio ($r$) are sensitive to the **spectral distribution of species-dependent memory accumulation**. Specifically, if the memory load $Q_{mem}$ is partitioned into a "heavy" sector (high-energy/short-wavelength modes) and a "light" sector (low-energy/long-wavelength modes), the backreaction on the condensate will be non-homogeneous. 

By introducing a **multi-component memory feedback model** where $Q_{mem} = \sum_{i=1}^{N_s} \omega_i Q_i$, where $\omega_i$ represents the coupling strength of the $i$-th species to the graviton condensate, we can decouple the stabilization of the Hubble scale from the generation of tensor perturbations. I propose to test the hypothesis that a **hierarchical species distribution** (where only a subset of species contributes to the memory burden at any given time $t$) allows for a "graceful exit" that is triggered by the saturation of specific high-coupling species, rather than the total $N_s$. This will be investigated by numerically solving the coupled system with a time-varying $N_s(t)$ that mimics the sequential decoupling of species as $H$ evolves, aiming to simultaneously satisfy $N_e \geq 60$ and $r < 0.03$ without fine-tuning the power-law index of $F(x)$.
